Intro to the Exposure Triangle

If you are new to photography, you are probably still trying to wrap your head around what exposure means. Simply put, exposure refers to the amount of light that reaches your camera sensor or film. Light will determine the quality and mood of your photograph. Thus it is essential to understand how exposure works. The best way to begin to master exposure is by understanding the exposure triangle.

As Alex Cooke expalins in his Fstoppers article: "Don't worry, this isn't a geometry lesson. The exposure triangle is a common way of associating the three variables that determine the exposure of a photograph: aperture, shutter speed, and ISO."

The Triangle

Apeture

Aperture is the size of the opening through which light enters the camera. How small or big this opening can be is determined by the type of lens you are using. We define the size of the aperture in f-stops. A smaller f-stop number means a larger aperture. Which means that f/1.4 is a bigger opening than f/22.

Aperture is important to master because it determines the depth of field of your photograph. The depth of field refers to how much of the background is in focus or blurred. Using a shallow depth of field means setting a large aperture (like f/1.4) to get a blurred background and an isolated subject. Using a deep depth of field means setting a small aperture (like f/22) to capture a fully sharp image. Shallow depths of fields are commonly used for portraits, whereas deep depth of fields are used for landscape photography.

Shutter Speed

The shutter speed of your camera controls how long lights come into the camera. The more time the shutter remains open, the more light enters the camera. Shutter speed also affects how your photograph captures motion. A slower shutter speed will create a photograph with motion blur, and a fast shutter speed will freeze the subject.

If you are working with a shutter speed below 1/60th of a second, a tripod is recommended so that any motion comes from the subject and not from the photographer’s hands.

When setting the shutter to “BULB” mode you can manually keep your shutter open for longer than the standard camera settings. In these cases we recommend to use a remote along with the tripod, to avoid motion blur from directly pressing your camera’s shutter button.

ISO

ISO is difficult to explain in terms of digital cameras, but for film cameras it is basically the amount of chemicals on the film being used. The stronger the chemicals, the higher the ISO number, and the brighter the photograph. The lower the ISO number, the darker the photograph. ISO remains important for digital photography, and also controls how dark or bright your digital image will be.

You may think that higher ISO numbers are better because they result in brighter photographs. However, the problem with using a high ISO is that your photo will have more grain. Lower ISO numbers are recommended, because they result in high quality images. That being said, when photographing dark subjects, such as the stars at night, you may need a high ISO. A good rule is to control the light first with the shutter speed and aperture, and then set a higher ISO if the image is still too dark.

This table lists common ISO settings, the amount of grain they produce, and their best uses.
ISO Number Grain Amount Recommended Use
100 and 200 Low Use for brightly lit subjects, such as one standing outside in a sunny day.
400 and 800 Low to Medium Use for indoor subjects or subjects under poor light.
1600, 3200, and 6400 High Use for night shots or for subjects standing in the dark.
